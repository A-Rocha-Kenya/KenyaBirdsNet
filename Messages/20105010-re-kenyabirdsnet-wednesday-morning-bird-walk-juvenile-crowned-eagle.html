After quite a time meditating above Zimmerman pages 86f, 96f, 100f and 308f, I agree with Adam Kennedy, it was a Ayres's Hawk-Eagle.

Let's start with the head:


Juvenile African Crowned Eagle: “Pale buff or whitish head [...] grey eyes” Adult: “pale yellow eyes” (p.96)
Juvenile Ayres's Hawk-Eagle: “buff or pale rufous [...] face” (p.100) “Pale rufous supercilliary stripes usually present [...] eyes pale grey brown” (308)
The bill shown in the drawing of Adult African Crowned Eagle (p.97) is indeed enormous -- ours is less impressive.
So this point goes to Ayres's, I think.

On the opposite side:

Juvenile African Crowned Eagle: “black-spotted leg and tarsal feathers” (p.96)
The spots we saw on the breast clearly stop before the legs – all the rest including light yellow toes etc. is pretty similar.
Another point to Ayres's.
 
Finally if we have a look under his (or her? although males tend to have a more whitish face, while females tend to be darker in adults at least – let's assume it is a he) … under /his/ wings:



There is some blur due to the movement (he has not learned yet to pause during flight for the photographer's sake), still we'd expect stronger contrast in the mid-wing bands if it was an immature African Crowned Eagle (p.87).

Finally 3:0 for the Ayres's the preference of moister habitat might add another half a point …
