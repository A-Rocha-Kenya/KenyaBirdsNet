Yes Washington and others. Frustrating is to say the least! So this must must be around what was the (past tense) Busia grasslands Important Birds' Area. I am not sure these poor beings are trapped (by the way there is also that element in that part of the country employing specialist, properly crafted snap traps targeting raptors and noses for smaller birds) or poisoned. I wish there was an up-close footage of the event but if these are poisoned, I never came across poisoned (Barn) Swallows; not in my experience with deliberate poison-poaching at the rice schemes. But I know another poisoning tactic that kills anything and everything - dermal-acting queletox (Fenthion) spraying and this is the season for the not well thought out, arguably successful food securing technique and Barn Swallows would likely fall victim. This is the time of the year that the spraying goes down at least in Bunyala and some littoral fringes of the lake Victoria just west and north to Sio Port that is tens of kilometres from Alupe.

A part of me feels like it is a war that may never be won, sadly just because birds are small, insignificant, non-big five feathered creatures. How do you deal with something that once a minister for wildlife has laughed at? Just because citizens in a part of the country are stuck in the old ways of consuming wild birds and grossly using the most bizarre unsustainable methods to procure them. Would you be sure of a victory when all that should matter do not mill around you and back you up? My long experience leans on the negative.

Humble regards

Hi Washington,

As Adam mentions on the post, it looks like a poisoning event, something which is, unfortunately, all too common around Busia.  Martin Odino has been working on the poisoning issue in Western Kenya for years now and continues to be frustrated with a lack of political will to help solve the issue, and a lack of understanding on the part of the perpetrators that the poisons will affect their, and their children's, own health.  From my understanding, poison is laid out to kill birds that might be pests on rice.  Poisoned birds are often collected and taken home to be eaten.  Perhaps, if Martin is here on Kenya Birds Net he can comment further.  It's an ongoing struggle, and one that results in the death of large numbers of birds of all types every year.
