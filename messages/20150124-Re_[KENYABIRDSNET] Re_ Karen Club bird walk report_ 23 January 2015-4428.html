On the subject of Scarce Swifts, I had the following conversation with James Bradley, who lived in the Loresho area for many years and maintains a list for the location, in September 2012. Of course swifts can be very difficult, and I could be totally wrong about the identification, but it was an intriguing sighting. I take the point Fleur that the white rump of the White-rumped Swift can be difficult to see, especially in overcast conditions, but I scanned the flock for quite a long time and could not find a white rump at all. With best regards, JD

Dear James,

 

An interesting sighting over my home in Loresho this morning – a flock of about 15-20 small, darkish swifts with a very pointed tail, deeply forked when opened, and with slightly paler throats. The flock was silent, and was over my house for close to half an hour (though the overcast sky made it frustratingly difficult to get a very clear view).

 

I would say with some confidence that they were Scarce Swifts, with which I was familiar during the time I lived in Tigoni. They had no white on the rump, and were too dark and slim to be Palm Swifts. The smallish size and deeply forked tail rules out most other things. The only query I would have is the size of the flock, which is unusual for Scarce Swifts, which are usually more solitary, though I can’t think what else they might be.

 

All best, JD

Dear John,

 

Good to hear from you and thanks for passing on this sighting. From your description they sound like Scarce Swift and a very nice record for Nairobi. Interestingly, I was corresponding with Don Turner about this species a while ago and he had the following to say …

 

"Scarce Swift is even more of an enigma.  In southern Africa (Malawi & Zimbabwe) it is very much an intra-African migrant Oct - April and some of these almost certainly reach southern Tanzania, but how far north they come is unknown.  Kenya and n. Tanzania birds may be largely resident, but records are low for the May-September period, while records from South Sudan and Ethiopia are only May-Sept.  Does this suggest that our birds move north during the cold season, or do they go south ??

 

Your observation would appear to fit well with birds on passage from Ethiopia (perhaps even Kenya) southwards and the fact that you had a good number together also suggests migrants. I'll forward your sighting on to him as I know he will be interested.

 

I'll add this one to the area checklist and please send along anything else of note.

 

Best, James

Hi Patrick & Mike, 

Thanks for those comments. Patrick, I agree that the Brown-backed Woodpecker is fairly common throughout the forested areas of Karen, and elsewhere in Nairobi.

To expand on my comments about it being more common around Nairobi than is supposed, I think it is one of a group of species that can be underreported because it has some resemblance to a similar species that is more common - in this case, the Cardinal Woodpecker - and it takes a certain level of awareness by even experienced birders to sort them out. 

Others in this category could include Thick-billed Seedeater (Streaky Seedeater), Red-faced Cisticola (Singing Cisticola), Scarce Swift (Palm Swift), Collared Sunbird (Variable Sunbird), and Slender-billed Greenbul (various canopy warblers). 

An interesting point for discussion!!

All best, JD
