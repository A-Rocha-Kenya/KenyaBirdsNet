Thanks once again Nate,
The December to February records are something to worry about. It is
unfortunate that not one is accompanied by an image. Simon's Jan
record of birds calling on the South Coast with confirmation of the
recording taken by James Bradley as Mangrove would seem irrefutable,
Doris on her home patch at Nguni has seen enough MK's in her time.

There is one issue which cannot be tested without images, and it might
seem insulting, but immature Grey-headed Kingfishers after leaving the
nest take a while to acquire the chestnut underparts and start off
plain below. In fact at this stage they do not look like GHK with
their whitish underparts. They are also found on the coastal fringe
where resident (race hyacinthina). Of course GHK has a blackish centre
to the back whilst MK have a bluish-grey back. Visitors could be
misled by juv GHK if unfamiliar with our avifauna.
But then again maybe the odd bird in some years doesn't go back.

Then as we see, we have someone like Clive who studied birds on the
coast for a number of consecutive years and never had one out of their
normal window of appearance.

So I think these records are exceptions rather than any rule, I don't
know how other people read into this.

It would be good if Colin Jackson could help with the Bird Log at
Arocha, and Neil Baker for the situation as per the Tanzanian Atlas,
both of these would weigh heavily to support the exact status one way
or the other,

What I do find very intriguing is that when our birds are present, is
exactly the time they winter in KZN where they do not breed, which
certainly supports there being two different populations. When our
birds go south, so do the KZN birds to breed in the Eastern Cape. It's
dangerous to suppose anything in birds nowadays, but it would suggest
that our birds do not go as far south as Natal and are southern
Tanzanian and Mozambican birds.
